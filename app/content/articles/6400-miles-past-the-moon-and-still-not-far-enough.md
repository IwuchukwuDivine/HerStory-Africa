On April 1st, 2026, Christina Koch climbed into the Orion spacecraft alongside commander Reid Wiseman, pilot Victor Glover, and mission specialist Jeremy Hansen. Five days later, she flew 6,400 miles beyond the far side of the Moon. She became the first woman to travel beyond low Earth orbit. The farthest any woman has ever been from the planet she was born on.

She already held the record for the longest single spaceflight by a woman. 328 consecutive days aboard the International Space Station. She conducted the first all-female spacewalk. She holds degrees in electrical engineering and physics. She was selected as a NASA astronaut from a pool of over 6,100 applicants. She performed six spacewalks totalling over 42 hours. She spent a winter stationed at the South Pole doing research in some of the harshest conditions on Earth.

On April 10th, she returned safely. History had been made. And across the internet, thousands of people responded with the same handful of ideas.

_Who would make the sandwiches?_

## What the comments said

These are real comments from TikTok, under posts celebrating Christina Koch's mission. They are not cherry-picked outliers. They are top comments, many with hundreds or thousands of likes.

"Her husband still waiting for his sandwich."

"Also the farthest from a kitchen."

"Instead of pre packed food, now they can finally have fresh made sandwiches."

"Why the woman?"

"Hopefully the woman not in control." Over a thousand likes.

"Women so they don't get bored in space?"

"Imagine your wife going around the Moon with 3 dudes. Poor fella." Nearly eight thousand likes.

"Woman is going to be the first one to complain in space."

"Must we praise everything a woman does?"

"Her man is the real mvp for staying at home with the kids while she has fun in the stars."

A woman travels around the Moon after years of training, after a career spent at the frontier of human capability, and the response is that the real hero is the man who stayed home.

## What this actually reveals

The comments are not really about Christina Koch. They are about what her achievement triggers in people who have built their understanding of the world on the idea that women are less. Less capable. Less serious. Less worthy of the big moments.

When a woman does something that makes that belief impossible to maintain, the reflex is not to update the belief. The reflex is to diminish the woman. Drag her back to the kitchen. Sexualise her. Reduce her to somebody's wife. Question why she is there at all.

"Why the woman?" is perhaps the most revealing comment. Not "why this particular astronaut" or "is she qualified" but "why the woman?" As if three men on a Moon mission is natural and one woman makes it suspicious.

There is always a follow-up response to this kind of criticism. "It's just jokes." "Stop being so sensitive." But think about what the jokes are actually saying. They are saying that a woman's greatest possible achievement is still less important than her availability to make food for a man. They are saying that a woman in a professional setting with male colleagues is primarily a sexual object. They are saying that a woman who excels at the highest level of a field is still, fundamentally, in the wrong place.

These are not neutral jokes. They carry a message. And that message is received by every girl who reads them. Every girl watching the Artemis mission and thinking "I want to do that one day" will also see thousands of people saying that a woman doing exactly that is ridiculous, laughable, or wrong.

The jokes are not harmless. They are the atmosphere that girls grow up breathing.

## The pattern is old

Christina Koch is not the first woman to do something extraordinary and be met with mockery.

When Valentina Tereshkova became the first woman in space in 1963, the press spent more time discussing her gender than her mission. It took 19 years before another woman flew in space.

When women across Africa have taken on leadership roles throughout history, the reaction has followed the same script. [Funmilayo Ransome-Kuti](/women/funmilayo-ransome-kuti) was called a troublemaker. [Winnie Madikizela-Mandela](/women/winnie-madikizela-mandela) was called dangerous. [Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie](/women/chimamanda-ngozi-adichie) was called angry. From [Yaa Asantewaa](/women/yaa-asantewaa) leading an army against the British to [Ellen Johnson Sirleaf](/women/ellen-johnson-sirleaf) becoming Africa's first elected female head of state, the pattern holds. Disbelief, mockery, then quiet acceptance only after the achievement becomes impossible to deny.

The words change with the era. The purpose stays the same. Make the woman the problem so you never have to deal with what she has accomplished.

## What we should actually be talking about

Christina Koch's mission is a milestone for humanity. She is part of a crew paving the way for human beings to return to and eventually live beyond the Moon.

But her mission is also significant specifically because she is a woman. Not because women needed to prove they could do it. But because every barrier that falls creates space for the next person to walk through.

Somewhere right now, there is a girl watching clips of Christina Koch floating inside Orion, looking out at the Earth, and that girl is thinking: that could be me. That thought is worth more than every sandwich joke ever typed.

And somewhere right now, there is also a girl scrolling through those same comments and absorbing the message that even if she does the most extraordinary thing in the world, it will not be enough.

## What to take away

- Christina Koch is one of the most accomplished astronauts in history, with multiple records and decades of proven competence.
- The response to her Artemis II mission in online comment sections was overwhelmingly sexist, reducing her to domestic and sexual stereotypes.
- These comments are not harmless jokes. They carry a message that every girl who reads them absorbs.
- The fact that this reaction exists in 2026, to a woman orbiting the Moon, proves that the fight for equality is nowhere near over.
- Every girl who sees Christina Koch and thinks "that could be me" is the reason representation matters. Every comment that tries to drag her back down is the reason the work continues.

Christina Koch went to the Moon. She went farther from this planet than any woman in human history. And thousands of people could not see past the fact that she is a woman. That is not her limitation. It is theirs.
